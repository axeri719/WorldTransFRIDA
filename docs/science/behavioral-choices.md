### Overview

The Behavioral Choices module is the mechanism by which a scenario can override FRIDA's endogenous Behavioral Change, forcing externally specified policy assumptions onto the model in place of the behavior it would otherwise generate. It is deliberately lightweight: it holds no stocks or flows of its own, instead exposing a compact set of high-level switches and override trajectories that, when activated, replace the demand signals the behavioral dynamics would have produced. Its role is to be the interface between externally imposed behavioral assumptions and the substantive human modules — principally Land Use and Agriculture and Energy — that turn demand into production, emissions, and resource use.

Each behavioral domain is represented by a paired switch and a corresponding override trajectory. The module covers the choices with the largest climate footprint: total food demand, the share of animal products in the diet (including a separate aquatic-animal-products share), per-person energy use, and a general environmental-consciousness setting that scales how strongly pro-environmental motivations weigh on behavior. A shared start year determines when these settings take effect, so that behavioral assumptions can be introduced part-way through a simulation rather than from the outset.

### Relationship to the Behavioral Change module

Behavioral Choices sits on top of the dynamic Behavioral Change module and exists to override it. Behavioral Change supplies the *dynamics*: it endogenously evolves diet and food demand over time as a function of perceived accessibility, descriptive and personal norms, and perceived climate and consumption risk, all responding to feedback from the wider human–climate system. Behavioral Choices supplies the *override*: for each domain it decides whether the demand passed to the consuming module is the endogenously evolving value the dynamics would produce, a simpler income-driven counterfactual, or a directly specified policy trajectory that supersedes both. Switching a choice on therefore substitutes an external assumption for whatever behavior the model would otherwise have generated.

In practice this is implemented as a precedence rule inside the consuming modules. When a Behavioral Choices switch is active and the start year has passed, the override value it carries takes priority and is used directly. Otherwise demand falls back to the endogenous behavioral dynamics (or, where selected for comparison, to a GDP-driven formulation that ignores behavioral change). In this way Behavioral Choices sits in front of the behavioral dynamics as a controllable top layer without altering the dynamics themselves.

### Role in scenarios

Because the module concentrates the behavioral assumptions of a run into a few clearly labelled levers, it is the natural place to express different futures for human behavior. Leaving the switches off lets behavior emerge endogenously, so that diet and demand co-evolve with prices, norms, and climate. Turning a switch on lets a scenario impose a specific trajectory — for example a deliberate dietary shift away from animal products, a change in total caloric demand, or a target for per-person energy use — and observe how that prescribed behavior propagates through agriculture, land use, energy, emissions, and ultimately the climate. This separation keeps the substantive modules agnostic about where their demand signals come from, while giving scenario design a single, transparent set of controls for representing alternative behavioral assumptions.
