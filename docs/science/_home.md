FRIDA — the Feedback-based knowledge Repository for Integrated Assessments — is a global, one-region integrated assessment model that treats the human world and the Earth system as a single, fully coupled whole. Rather than passing information between a climate model and an economic model through fixed scenarios, FRIDA represents emissions, the climate's response, the resulting impacts, and the human reactions to those impacts as one continuous web of feedback. Emissions, warming, sea level, climate damages, and the economic, demographic, behavioral, and policy responses to them are all generated inside the model. There are no exogenous emission pathways and no prescribed growth trajectories: outcomes emerge from the structure itself.

## What makes FRIDA distinctive

Integrated assessment models come in many designs, and a great deal of valuable work already couples the human and climate systems — from detailed process IAMs that link energy–economy modules to climate emulators, to cost–benefit models that feed climate damages back into economic decisions. These frameworks have been enormously productive, and much of FRIDA's structure builds on the empirical relationships and modelling traditions they established. FRIDA's contribution is a matter of emphasis. Large-scale process IAMs are typically run with rich spatial and sectoral detail and explore the future through coordinated concentration and socioeconomic scenarios, an approach that is well suited to many questions and that often brackets climate-to-economy feedbacks rather than resolving every one of them endogenously. FRIDA makes those feedbacks its central object of study: it is designed so that climate damages continuously reshape the trajectory of the system that produced them, without relying on prescribed emission or growth pathways.

FRIDA closes that loop. It deliberately trades spatial and sectoral specificity for *feedback complexity*: the dynamics that arise when many interconnected processes act on one another. Built with the System Dynamics method, it expresses real-world processes as a coupled system of differential equations grounded in physical law, well-established empirical relationships, and leading theory from economics and environmental psychology. It is not an optimising model; instead of assuming perfectly rational agents, it simulates how people form and revise expectations and adjust behavior under uncertainty.

Because the whole model runs in seconds, FRIDA can be executed as large ensembles — sampling parametric uncertainty across the entire human–climate system — so that results carry explicit, quantified confidence bounds rather than a false sense of precision. Each subsystem is calibrated to historical observations from around 1980 onward and validated both for the behavior it reproduces and for the structural soundness of the processes generating it.

## What is endogenous, and what is set by scenario

The defining feature of FRIDA is how much it leaves to the model. Population, economic output, energy demand and the evolving fuel mix, agricultural production and land use, the carbon cycle, radiative forcing, temperature, sea level, climate impacts, dietary and transport behavior, and the financial system are all endogenous. The only external drivers are the natural ones — solar variation and volcanic forcing — plus a small set of policy levers. Carbon pricing, energy taxes and subsidies, support for carbon capture and removal, coastal adaptation spending, fiscal settings, and demand-side overrides are available as deliberate "what-if" experiments; even then, the human system responds to a policy rather than having an outcome imposed on it.

## The kinds of dynamics it produces

Run forward, FRIDA tells a coherent story about the coupled system. Through the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, self-reinforcing economic loops — investment, employment, wages, consumption, and fossil-fuel learning-by-doing — drive growth that runs largely free of biophysical limits. As warming and its damages accumulate, balancing feedbacks acting through labour productivity, mortality, infrastructure, agriculture, and financial stability progressively constrain that growth, often arriving only after the system has entered an impact-dominated regime. The model thus illuminates the interplay of growth, biophysical limits, behavioral adaptation, and the timing of policy — highlighting why early, feedback-targeted intervention matters, and where tightly coupled dynamics can unfold differently from what less coupled framings anticipate.
