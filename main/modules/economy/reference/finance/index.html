    Finance.lending_standards¶

    stock

    Units: dmnl · Dimensions: Run · Inflows: Finance.change_in_lending_standards

    Lending standards is the actual stock variable representing how tight or loose bank credit conditions are at a given moment. This stock increases from change in lending standard, with an initial value of 1, so the model starts from a normalized baseline credit stance. This variable matters because it is the implemented constraint on lending behavior, not just a desired signal, and it feeds into the wider macro-financial system. When lending standards rise, banks become more restrictive, which reduces bank investment growth and can also increase firm failures through refinancing pressure. So this is one of the main transmission variables through which climate-driven financial stress spreads into investment, output, and employment.

    Initial value: 

    1

    Finance.bank_investment_growth_rate¶

    Units: dmnl/year · Dimensions: Run

    It represents the actual bank investment growth. It accounts for (1) indicated bank investment growth due to long term solvency and reference bank investment growth, and (2) effect of lending standard on bank investment growth rate.

    Finance.climate_adjusted_failure_rate_when_default_originated¶

    aux

    Units: dmnl · Dimensions: Run

    It represents the historical failure environment embedded in the current loan portfolio. "What failure rate was in effect when the loans that are defaulting today were originally issued?"

    Loans that default today were not created today because they were created many years ago, under different climate and economic conditions. It allows default dynamics to depend on the worsening of conditions relative to origination rather than instantaneous climate risk.     Finance.perceived_defaults_of_risky_assets_for_lending_standards¶

    aux

    Units: b$/year · Dimensions: Run

    This variable is the amount of risky-asset defaults that banks perceive when deciding whether to tighten or loosen lending standards. Its equation is SMTH1(total risky asset defaults - (private insurance payouts + Central Bank insurance payout +government insurance payout), reporting delay), so it takes actual risky defaults, subtracts insurance coverage, and then smooths the result through a reporting delay. This matters because banks do not react to raw defaults instantly or perfectly; they react to a delayed and partially insured loss signal. The variable is therefore the behavioral credit-stress input used for prudential tightening, rather than a direct accounting measure of defaults.
